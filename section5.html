Section 5: The Wave Equation

The backgorund image is taken from Unsplash. It shows us that in nature, for instantce, the wave follows the behaviour of the wave equation. The simplest model of all is a string under tension. Using Newton's second law we can easily derive the wave equation.

In this section, there are three topics to be studied:

  • Derivation of the Wave Euqation
  • Solution by Fourier Series
  • Complex Initial Conditions

    • Derivation of the Wave Equation

    We will use Newton's seond law to derive the wave equation. The following image is taken from Hyper physics. We will begin the analysis by stating several assumptions:

    1. Constant \(\rho\), which is also called mass per unit length (\(kg/m\));
    2. \(T >> mg\) or the tension applided is much greater than the gravity;
    3. Small \(U\) or deflection.

    $$ \begin{cases} \sum{F_x} = T_2cos{\beta}-T_1cos{\alpha}=0 \\ \sum{F_y} = T_2sin{\beta}-T_1sin{\alpha}= m a \end{cases} $$ The mass and accelaration can be replaced as: $$ m a = \rho\delta{x}\frac{\partial^2{u}}{\partial t^2} \\ \Rrightarrow \begin{cases} \sum{F_x} = T_2cos{\beta}-T_1cos{\alpha}=0 && [1] \\ \sum{F_y} = T_2sin{\beta}-T_1sin{\alpha}= \rho\delta{x}\frac{\partial^2{u}}{\partial t^2} && [2] \end{cases} $$ Use equation \([2]\div[1]\), to obtain: $$ \begin{align} \frac{\rho\delta{x}}{T}\frac{\partial^2{u}}{\partial t^2} & = \frac{T_2sin{\beta}}{T_2cos{beta}} - \frac{T_1sin{\alpha}}{T_1cos{\alpha}} \\ \Rightarrow \frac{\rho\delta{x}}{T}\frac{\partial^2{u}}{\partial t^2} & = tan{\beta} - tan{\alpha} \\ \Rightarrow \frac{\rho}{T}\frac{\partial^2{u}}{\partial t^2} & = \frac{1}{\delta{x}}(tan{\beta} - tan{\alpha}) \end{align} $$ In the diagram, it has indicated that we can replace the tangent with the derivative: $$ \frac{\rho}{T}\frac{\partial^2{u}}{\partial t^2} = \frac{1}{\delta{x}}(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}|_{x+ \Delta x} - \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}|_x ) $$ As \(x\rightarrow \infty\), we can say: $$ \frac{1}{\delta{x}}(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}|_{x+ \Delta x} - \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}|_x ) = \frac{\partial^2u}{\partial t^2} \\ \Rightarrow \frac{\rho}{T}\frac{\partial^2{u}}{\partial t^2} = \frac{\partial^2u}{\partial t^2} $$ By rearranging the term, we have the Wave Equation: $$ \frac{\partial ^2 U}{\partial t^2 } = {c^2 } \frac{\partial^2 U}{\partial x^2} \qquad \text{where} \qquad c^{2} = \frac{T}{\rho} $$

    It has been also determined that the class of the wave euqation is Hyperbolic. This will be left as an exercise.

    Solution by Fourier Series

    Q: It is given to us the following conditions. A standard Fender guitar string length is 0.65m with a typical tension of 71N. The typical mass/unit lenth of the first string is \(0.401\times10^{-3} kg/m\). If I pull the string into a triangle shape:

    Which also satisfies the following conditions: $$ f(x) = \left\{ \begin{array}{c} \frac{2k}{L}x & \text{if}& 0< x < \frac{L}{2} \\ \\ \frac{2k}{L}(L-x) & \text{if}& \frac{L}{2} < x < L \end{array} \right. $$

    Now we also need the Initial Conditions (I.C.) and Boundary Conditions (B.C.) to help us resolve the problem: $$u(x,0) = f(x) \qquad u(0,t) = 0 \qquad u(L,t) = 0 $$ Remember, there is noe data you need tocalculate. That is the \(c^{2}\): $$\rho = 0.401\times10^{-3} kg/m \qquad T = 71 N $$ $$ \Rightarrow c^{2} = \frac{T}{\rho} = 177057.3566 (m^{2}/s^{2}) $$

    It is now a good time to refresh the memory of How to Find the Solution to a PDE:

    1. Define all of the parameters needed to solve the question;
    2. Use Separation of Variables to separate the variables;
    3. Solve the resulting ODE for F(x);
    4. Solve the resulting ODE for G(t);
    5. Full solution to the question.

    With the rules kept in mind, it is very easy for us to get started!

    We have successfully defined our parameters for soling the question. Thus, we move to the second step: Separation of Variables.

    Step 2: Separation of Variables

    • Assume a solution: \(U(x,t)=F(x)*G(t)\)

      Subsititute this into the wave equation: $$ FG_t - c^{2}GF_{xx} = 0 \\ \frac{G_t}{c^2G}=\frac{F_{xx}}{F} \\ \text{Set the above expression to a constant} -p^{2} \\ \Rightarrow \frac{G_t}{c^2G}=\frac{F_{xx}}{F} = -p^2 \\ \text{Now, we are able to find the solution for each ODE} $$

      • Please note, the two ODEs are \(\frac{G_t}{c^2G}=-p^2\) and \(\frac{F_{xx}}{F}=-p^2\).

    • Thus, for the Spatial Term : $$ F_n(x)=a_ncos{(px)}+b_nsin{(px)} $$ We now are going back to the B.Cs, \(u(0,t)=0\) and \(u(L,t)=0\) which also means that \(F_n(0)=0\) at these points.

      Hence, we can find out that \(a_n=0\) and \(p=\frac{n\pi}{L}\) where \(n=1,2,3,...\)

      Thus, we have successully obtained the solution for \(F_n(x)\): $$ F_n(x)=b_nsin{(\frac{n \pi x}{L})} $$

    • For the Temporal Term: $$ G_n(t)= B_ncos{(\lambda_nt)}+B^{*}_nsin{(\lambda_nt)} \\\text{where} \qquad \lambda_n=\frac{cn\pi}{L} \qquad \& \qquad B_n,B^{*}_n \qquad \text{are constants} $$ Thus, one solution to the current ICs and BCs becomes: $$ u_n(x,t)=F_n(x)*G_n(t)= sin{(\frac{n \pi x}{L})}(B_ncos{(\lambda_nt)}+B^{*}_nsin{(\lambda_nt)}) $$ Note: the constant for \(F_n(x)\) has been combined into \(G_n(t)\).
    • Eigenfunction and Eigenvalues:

      Q:What is eigenfunction and eigenvalue?

      An:

      Mathematical definition: an eigenfunction of an operator \(\hat{A}\) is a function \(f\) such that the application of \(\hat{A}\) on \(f\) gives \(f\) times a constant. $$ \hat{A}f = kf $$

      Let's decipher this together.

      What is the definition actually saying? Let's use \(e\) as an example. As we all know, that the derivative of \(e\) is itself. That is: $$ \text{if} \qquad f(x)=e^{2x} \\ \text{then} \qquad f'(x)=2e^{2x} $$ Well, that seems to be pointless. But if we go back to the definition, we can see in our case of \(f(x)\). The operator, here, becomes 1.Or \(\hat{A}=2\) \(\Rightarrow \hat{A}f(x)=2*f(x)\).

      In another way saying this is: $$ \text{if let} \qquad \lambda = 2 \Rightarrow f(x)=e^{\lambda x} \\ \text{then} \qquad 2f(x)=\lambda f(x) \\ $$ Thus, we say \(\lambda\) is the eigenvalue of the eignefunction \(f(x)=e^{\lambda x} = e^{2x}\).

      Remember, you should never talk about an eigenvalue without refering back to your eigenfunciton. The eigenvalues and the eigenfunctions are properties of the specific system. This is also somewhat different from eigenvector in matrix.

      Thus, in our problem. We say "solution \(u_n\) is an eigenfunction of the problem, correpsonding to the eigenvalue \(\lambda _n\)".

      In this particular case, we can now have more definitions upon the mode n:

      • n=1 mode is called the fundamental mode.
      • Other modes are called overstones or harmonics.
      • Each mode has n nodes, where the string does not move.
      • Frequency is: \(\frac{\lambda _n}{2 \pi} = \frac{cn}{2L} \)
      • The set \(\{\lambda_1,\lambda_2,...\}\) is called the spectrum.
      • The nth normal mode has n-1 nodes.

    • Full Solution:

      As is the case from Heat Equation, all values of n are solutions to the euqation.

      Thus the complete solution is a combination of all solutions:

      $$ u(x,t)=\sum^{\infty}_{n=1}=\sum^{\infty}_{n=1}(B_ncos{(\lambda_n t)}+B^{*}_nsin{(\lambda_nt)})sin{\frac{n\pi x}{L}} $$

      Refering back to the initial conditions, that is \(u(x,0)=f(x)\)

      $$ u(x,0)=f(x)=\sum^{\infty}_{n=1}B_nsin{\frac{n\pi x}{L}} $$

      The above equation has one coefficient \(B_n\). We call it the Fourier Series Coefficient. It can be solved using Euler's formula.

      Remember the definition of eigenfunction and eigenvalue? We now also need to keep this is mind: If the eigenvalue is not present the initial conditions, it will never appear!

      Now, let's take a look at the derivative: $$ \frac{\partial{u}}{\partial{t}} = \sum^{\infty}_{n=1}(-B_n\lambda_nsin{(\lambda_nt)}+B^*_n\lambda_ncos{(\lambda_nt)})sin{\frac{n\pi x}{L}} $$ At \(t=0\): $$ \frac{\partial{u}}{\partial{t}}|_{\scriptstyle{t=0}} =B_n^*\lambda_nsin{\frac{n\pi x}{L}} $$ The first derivative reflects the velocity of the position. We know that at \(t=0\) this string is at stationary. Thus: $$ \frac{\partial{u}}{\partial{t}}|_{\scriptstyle{t=0}}=0 \Rightarrow B_n^*=0 \quad \text{Since}\quad \sin(\frac{n\pi x}{L})\quad \text{is oscillating between -1 and 1.} $$ Thus, the final siolution is now: $$ u(x,t)=\sum^{\infty}_{n=1}B_ncos{(\lambda_n t)}sin{\frac{n\pi x}{L}} \quad\text{where}\quad \lambda_n=\frac{cn\pi}{L} \quad n =1,2,3,... \quad\& \quad c^2 = \frac{T}{\rho} $$

    Complex Initial Conditions

    Just a quick remind of the initial condition:

    $$u(x,0) = f(x)$$ Which the f(x) is: $$ f(x) = \left\{ \begin{array}{c} \frac{2k}{L}x & \text{if}& 0< x < \frac{L}{2} \\ \\ \frac{2k}{L}(L-x) & \text{if}& \frac{L}{2} < x < L \end{array} \right. $$ Again, our full solution is: $$ u(x,t)=\sum^{\infty}_{n=1}B_ncos{(\lambda_n t)}sin{\frac{n\pi x}{L}} \quad\text{where}\quad \lambda_n=\frac{cn\pi}{L} \quad n =1,2,3,... \quad\& \quad c^2 = \frac{T}{\rho} $$ Now, a another quick remind for Fourier coefficients of \(f(x)\) which is given by the Euler Formulas (\(\frac{\pi}{L}\) in \(dx\) cancels \(\frac{1}{\pi}\)):

    (Ps: Please read the textbook P483-488)

    $$ \begin{align} a_0 &= \frac{1}{2L} \int^{L}_{-L}f(x)dx \\ a_n &= \frac{1}{L} \int^{L}_{-L}f(x) cos{\frac{n\pi x}{L}}dx \quad n = 1,2,3,... \\ b_n &= \frac{1}{L} \int^{L}_{-L}f(x) sin{\frac{n\pi x}{L}}dx \quad n = 1,2,3,... \end{align} $$ With the above formula kept in mind, we can derive the expression for \(B_n\). However, before we attempt to solve the complex expression, there are several important observations to be made.

    We can see that \(f(x)\) is and odd function (\(f(-x)=-f(x)\), at least on the interval of \(0< x < \frac{L}{2}\)). This means that we can treat the Fourier coefficient as a Fourier sine series. That is:

    $$ \begin{align} B_n & = \frac{1}{L} \int^{L}_{-L}f(x) sin{\frac{n\pi x}{L}}dx \\& = \frac{2}{L} \int^{L}_{0}f(x) sin{\frac{n\pi x}{L}}dx \\& = \frac{2}{L} \int^{\frac{L}{2}}_{0}\frac{2k}{L}x*sin{\frac{n\pi x}{L}}dx + \int^{L}_{\frac{L}{2}}\frac{2k}{L}(L-x)*sin{\frac{n\pi x}{L}}dx \end{align} $$ By observing the shape of the function \(f(x)\), we can see it can be separated into two equal areas. We use this to change our integration boundary agin to: $$ \begin{align} B_n & = \frac{2}{L} \int^{\frac{L}{2}}_{0}\frac{2k}{L}x*sin{\frac{n\pi x}{L}}dx + \int^{L}_{\frac{L}{2}}\frac{2k}{L}(L-x)*sin{\frac{n\pi x}{L}}dx \\& = \frac{4}{L} \int^{\frac{L}{2}}_{0}\frac{2k}{L}x*sin{\frac{n\pi x}{L}}dx \end{align} $$ Now, the very last thing to yield the solution is to calculate out \(B_n\).

    Use the method of differentiation by parts: $$ \int u \frac{dv}{dx} dx =uv - \int v \frac{du}{dx}dx $$ In our case, we choose \(u=x, v'= sin{\frac{n\pi x}{L}} \). We now have: $$ \begin{align} u &= x && u' =1dx \\ v' &= sin{(\frac{n\pi x}{L})} \Rightarrow && v = -\frac{L}{n\pi}cos{(\frac{n\pi x}{L})} \end{align} $$ So: $$ \begin{align} B_n & = \frac{4}{L} \int^{\frac{L}{2}}_{0}\frac{2k}{L}x*sin{\frac{n\pi x}{L}}dx \\& = \frac{8k}{L^2} \{(x*-\frac{L}{n\pi}cos{(\frac{n\pi x}{L})})|^{\frac{L}{2}}_0 - \int^{\frac{L}{2}}_{0} -\frac{L}{n\pi}cos{(\frac{n\pi x}{L})} dx\} \\& = \frac{8k}{L^2}(\int^{\frac{L}{2}}_{0} \frac{L}{n\pi}cos{(\frac{n\pi x}{L})} dx) \\& = \frac{8k}{L^2} \frac{L}{n\pi} \int^{\frac{L}{2}}_{0} cos{(\frac{n\pi x}{L})} dx \\& = \frac{8k}{L^2} \frac{L}{n\pi} \frac{L}{n\pi} sin{(\frac{n\pi x}{L})}|^{\frac{L}{2}}_0 \\& = \frac{8k}{n^2 \pi ^2} sin{\frac{n\pi}{2}} \quad \text{where} \quad n = 1,2,3,... \end{align} $$ That is, our final full solution to this question is: $$ u(x,t)=\sum^{\infty}_{n=1} \frac{8k}{n^2 \pi ^2} sin{\frac{n\pi}{2}} cos{(\lambda_n t)}sin{\frac{n\pi x}{L}} \quad\text{where}\quad \lambda_n=\frac{cn\pi}{L} \quad n =1,2,3,... \quad\& \quad c^2 = \frac{T}{\rho} $$ Be aware of the term \(sin\), as it takes the value -1 and 1. $$ u(x,t) = \frac{8k}{\pi ^2}(\frac{1}{1^2}sin{\frac{\pi}{L}xcos{\frac{\pi c}{L}}t} - \frac{1}{3^2}sin{\frac{3\pi}{L}xcos{\frac{3\pi c}{L}}t+-...} ) $$
